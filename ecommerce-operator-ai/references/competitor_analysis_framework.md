# Competitor Analysis Framework

Use this reference to convert competitor links, CSV rows, screenshots, or manual notes into decisions.

## Data to Capture

| Dimension | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Identity | competitor name, URL, platform, brand/store, category |
| Offer | price, coupons, bundles, shipping, return promise |
| Trust | rating, review count, review quality, badges, seller age |
| Content | title keywords, bullet structure, detail page sections, FAQ |
| Product | materials, specs, compatibility, included accessories, variants |
| Image/video | hero angle, lifestyle scenes, infographics, comparison, UGC |
| Demand signals | repeated review praise, repeated complaints, Q&A topics |
| Risks | trademark use, exaggerated claims, certification claims, unsafe visuals |

## Scoring Model

Score each competitor 1-5:

- `Price pressure`: 5 means hard to beat on price.
- `Content quality`: 5 means listing is polished and keyword-rich.
- `Feature strength`: 5 means product has strong functional advantages.
- `Review moat`: 5 means high rating and review count are difficult to overcome.
- `Image strength`: 5 means images clearly increase conversion.

Then identify:

- **Must match**: baseline features customers expect.
- **Can beat**: weaknesses visible in reviews, images, copy, shipping, bundle, or service.
- **Should avoid**: price wars, risky claims, copied visuals, low-margin bundles.

## Report Structure

1. Executive summary: market situation in 3-5 bullets.
2. Competitor table: URL, price, rating, review count, claims, strengths, weaknesses.
3. Opportunity map: content gap, product gap, image gap, service gap.
4. Listing recommendations: title keywords, bullets, description sections.
5. Image recommendations: hero/lifestyle/callout/UGC ideas.
6. Action plan: priority, impact, owner, evidence needed.

## Review Mining Heuristics

- Repeated praise becomes a required selling point.
- Repeated complaints become a differentiation opportunity only if the user's product truly solves them.
- Low-frequency complaints should not dominate positioning unless they match a severe safety or quality issue.
- Never claim competitors have defects unless evidence is cited from user-provided data or public sources checked during the task.
